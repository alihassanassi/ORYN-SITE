What to Expect from Your First Penetration Test

Published March 8, 2026 · 7 min read · By ORYN Security

A penetration test (or "pentest") is one of the most valuable investments a business can make in its security. But if you've never had one done, the process can seem intimidating. Here's what actually happens — and what you'll get out of it.

What Is a Penetration Test?

A penetration test is an authorized, simulated cyberattack against your systems. A skilled security professional (the pentester) attempts to find and exploit vulnerabilities in your network, applications, and infrastructure — using the same techniques real attackers would use.

The key difference: the pentester works for you. They document everything they find, explain the risk, and give you specific guidance on how to fix it.

Types of Penetration Tests

  • Network Penetration Test: Tests your network infrastructure — firewalls, routers, servers, workstations. Looks for misconfigurations, open ports, vulnerable services.
  • Web Application Penetration Test: Tests your web applications for OWASP Top 10 vulnerabilities — SQL injection, XSS, authentication flaws, etc.
  • Wireless Penetration Test: Tests your WiFi networks for weak encryption, rogue access points, and network segmentation issues.
  • Social Engineering: Tests your people — phishing campaigns, pretexting calls, physical security testing.
  • Red Team Assessment: A full-scope simulation of a real attack, combining multiple techniques over an extended period.

The Process: Step by Step

1. Scoping & Planning

Before anything happens, you'll discuss what's being tested, what's off-limits, and what the goals are. You'll sign a statement of work and rules of engagement. This is critical — a pentest without proper authorization is just hacking.

2. Reconnaissance

The pentester gathers information about your organization — publicly available data, DNS records, employee information, technology stack. This mirrors what a real attacker would do before launching an attack.

3. Vulnerability Discovery

Using a combination of automated scanning and manual testing, the pentester identifies potential vulnerabilities in your systems. This goes far beyond running a vulnerability scanner — experienced pentesters find issues that automated tools miss.

4. Exploitation

The pentester attempts to exploit discovered vulnerabilities to demonstrate real-world impact. Can they access sensitive data? Can they move laterally through your network? Can they escalate privileges to gain administrative access?

5. Reporting

You receive a detailed report that includes every finding, its severity, proof of exploitation, and specific remediation guidance. A good pentest report is actionable — it tells you exactly what to fix and how to fix it, prioritized by risk.

What Pentesters Typically Find

In our experience testing small and medium businesses, these are the most common findings:

  1. Default or weak credentials on network devices and applications
  2. Missing software patches on critical systems
  3. Flat network architecture with no segmentation
  4. Sensitive data stored without encryption
  5. Excessive user privileges (everyone is an admin)
  6. Missing MFA on email, VPN, and admin panels
  7. Outdated or misconfigured firewalls

How Much Does It Cost?

Penetration testing costs vary based on scope. For a small business:

  • Network pentest: $2,500 - $7,500
  • Web application pentest: $3,000 - $10,000
  • Full-scope assessment: $5,000 - $15,000

This is a fraction of the cost of the breach the test helps prevent. At ORYN, our security assessments start from $2,500 and our penetration testing from $3,500.

A pentest isn't a pass/fail exam. It's a map of where you're vulnerable and a prioritized plan for getting stronger. Every business that gets tested comes out more secure.
