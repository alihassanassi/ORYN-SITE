How to Build an Incident Response Plan Before You Need One

Published March 1, 2026 · 11 min read · By ORYN Security

You don't build a fire escape during a fire. And you don't build an incident response plan during a breach. Yet most small businesses have no documented plan for what to do when — not if — a security incident occurs.

Why You Need a Plan

When a breach happens, every minute counts. Without a plan, you get chaos: people making decisions under pressure with incomplete information, no clear chain of command, and no documented procedures. This leads to longer response times, greater damage, and worse outcomes.

A documented incident response plan reduces breach costs by an average of $2.66 million (IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report). For small businesses, the difference is often between surviving an incident and closing your doors.

The 6-Phase Incident Response Framework

Based on the NIST Incident Response framework, adapted for small businesses:

Phase 1: Preparation

This is everything you do before an incident occurs:

  • Document your critical assets — what systems and data are essential to your business?
  • Identify your incident response team — who gets called and what's their role?
  • Establish communication channels — how will the team coordinate during an incident?
  • Secure your tools — ensure you have forensic tools, backup access, and contact information for external resources (legal, insurance, law enforcement, IR firm)
  • Train your team — everyone should know the basics of incident identification and reporting

Phase 2: Identification

How do you know you've been breached? Common indicators:

  • Unusual system behavior or performance degradation
  • Unexpected account activity or privilege changes
  • Files being encrypted or modified unexpectedly
  • Alerts from security monitoring tools
  • Reports from employees about suspicious emails or activity
  • Notification from external parties (customers, partners, law enforcement)

Phase 3: Containment

Stop the bleeding. The goal is to limit the damage without destroying evidence:

  • Short-term containment: Isolate affected systems from the network immediately. Do NOT turn them off — you may need forensic data.
  • Evidence preservation: Take forensic images of affected systems before making changes.
  • Long-term containment: Apply temporary fixes to keep business running while you prepare for eradication.

Phase 4: Eradication

Remove the threat completely:

  • Identify and remove all malware, backdoors, and attacker persistence mechanisms
  • Patch the vulnerabilities that allowed the initial compromise
  • Reset all potentially compromised credentials
  • Rebuild compromised systems from clean images where necessary

Phase 5: Recovery

Restore normal operations carefully:

  • Restore systems from verified clean backups
  • Monitor restored systems closely for signs of reinfection
  • Gradually return systems to production with enhanced monitoring
  • Verify data integrity before full restoration

Phase 6: Lessons Learned

This is the phase most organizations skip — and it's the most important:

  • What happened? Full timeline of the incident.
  • What worked well in the response?
  • What could be improved?
  • What changes need to be made to prevent similar incidents?
  • Update the incident response plan based on lessons learned.

Your IR Contact List

At minimum, your plan should include contact information for:

  1. Internal incident response lead
  2. IT administrator / managed service provider
  3. Legal counsel (ideally with cybersecurity experience)
  4. Cyber insurance carrier (policy number and claims number)
  5. External incident response firm (like ORYN)
  6. Law enforcement contacts (FBI IC3, local field office)
  7. Communication lead (who talks to customers, media, regulators?)
The plan doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to exist. A simple, documented plan that everyone knows about is infinitely better than a perfect plan that nobody has read.
